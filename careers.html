WEEK 17 · APR 2025 5 min read

Data Center Classifications & Infrastructure — A Field Guide

MW
Manujaya Weerasinghe
Infrastructure Engineer, Diginext Scandinavia

Understanding how data centres are classified isn't just academic — it directly shapes how we engineer, install, and hand over every project. Here's how I think about it on site.

1. Ownership & Business Models

The first classification is about who owns what. There are four main models:

Enterprise
Internal IT — Maximum Control

Owned, operated and utilised by a single organisation (banks, telecoms). Full control over every layer. We see this most in financial sector builds.

Colocation (Colo)
Rented Space & Power

Organisations own the servers but rent the physical facility, cooling, and power. Our Kristiansand project is a colo fit-out — four data halls, client-owned hardware.

Managed Service
Outsourced Infrastructure

The provider owns and manages hardware, OS, and the facility. Reduces operational burden on the client. Common in SME and healthcare sectors.

Cloud
On-Demand Resources

IaaS, PaaS, SaaS delivered via the internet (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud). Maximum agility. Our Warsaw hyperscale project ultimately serves cloud providers at this layer.

2. Size & Scale: The Magnitude Progression

Physical scale determines cabling density, cooling strategy, and team size. From smallest to largest:

📦
Small / Server Rooms — Limited infrastructure, typically found localised in branch offices. Single-rack deployments. Our smart hands team handles these day-to-day in Stockholm.
🏢
Large Enterprise — Medium-to-large dedicated facilities serving one organisation. Structured cabling across multiple floors, containment systems, dedicated cooling zones.
🏭
Hyperscale — Massive global facilities housing thousands of servers, designed natively for extreme automation. Our Falun build is in this tier — 2024–2026, LEED Gold target. Cabling density and labelling discipline here is non-negotiable.

3. Infrastructure & Design Approaches

🏛️
Traditional

Physical on-site servers in a custom-built centralised facility.

Full structural control
📦
Modular

Pre-fabricated container modules containing IT, power, and cooling.

Rapid deployment & scalable
📡
Edge

Micro-facilities geographically close to end-users.

Ultra-low latency for 5G & IoT

4. Reliability: Uptime Tier Classifications

This is where infrastructure decisions have the most direct financial impact. The Uptime Institute's four tiers define how much redundancy — and therefore how much cabling and engineering complexity — is required.

99.67%
Tier I
Basic Capacity. Single path. No redundancy. Up to 28.8h downtime/yr.
99.75%
Tier II
Redundant capacity components. Up to 22h downtime/yr.
99.98%
Tier III
Concurrently maintainable. No shutdowns for maintenance. Our SLA target.
99.995%
Tier IV
Fault-tolerant. Capable of withstanding an unplanned event with zero impact.

5. Infrastructure Optimised by Workload

🖥️
HPC

Built for simulations, AI, and complex scientific computing.

Compute-Heavy
💾
Storage DCs

Optimised for long-term data storage, archiving, and backup.

Capacity-Heavy
🌐
Network DCs

Focused on routing, switching, and delivering telecom services.

Throughput-Heavy

Emerging Paradigms to Watch

🌿 Green Data Centers

Energy-efficient facilities powered entirely by renewable sources. Next-generation priority: liquid cooling, advanced airflow design, waste heat reuse.

⚙️ Software-Defined DCs (SDDC)

Virtualised infrastructure where networking, storage, and compute are entirely managed via software. Changes how we approach physical layer documentation and labelling.

Knowing these classifications means understanding what tier of redundancy your cabling path must support, what workload density your containment must handle, and what ownership model your documentation needs to satisfy. Every decision on site connects back to these fundamentals.

— Manujaya Weerasinghe, Infrastructure Engineer, Diginext Scandinavia

Confined space cable routing: what they don't teach in the classroom

AH
Anders Holm
Site Engineer, Kristiansand

On the Kristiansand colo project we had to route 4km of Cat6A through a sub-floor plenum measuring less than 600mm clearance — across 150 metres. Here's what actually worked.

Air Quality First: We deployed continuous O₂ monitoring before anyone went under the floor. CO₂ accumulation in sealed plenums is a real risk — not theoretical. Mandatory buddy system, entry/exit log, 15-minute rotation policy.

The technique: Standard cable-pulling with a fish tape was too slow and caused jacket damage. We switched to a nylon pull cord pre-run with a foam ball blower — cut installation time from ~18 hours to ~12 hours. That's 6 hours saved on a single floor zone.

Cable management: In confined spaces, organisation matters even more than in open areas. We used adhesive-backed J-hooks rated for the sub-floor environment, placed every 600mm. Every bundle was labelled at both ends before we went under — no relabelling in a confined space.

Key takeaway: Confined space work is 40% planning, 40% safety protocol, 20% actual cabling. The classroom teaches the cabling. Experience teaches the rest.
Week 15 · Apr 2025

Working at height in live data halls: our safety-first approach

SB
Sara Berg
Safety Lead, Falun

At the Falun hyperscale build, we're installing cable trays at 4.5m above a live equipment floor. Every lift requires a pre-work brief, MEWP inspection, and a two-person team. Here's our actual workflow.

01
Pre-work assessment: Walk the zone, identify live equipment directly below, confirm MEWP ground is rated for the load. If in doubt — no lift.
02
Tool tethering: Every tool above 1.8m is tethered. A dropped wrench from 4.5m can destroy a server and injure a colleague. This is non-negotiable on our sites.
03
Vibration isolation: Work near live servers requires vibration awareness. We use rubber-backed tray supports and avoid impact tools within 2m of operational equipment.
04
Alarm coordination: Before elevated work above active cooling units, we notify the NOC. Vibration can trip temperature sensors and cause false alarms — coordinate, don't surprise.
Key takeaway: Safety at height in live halls is about discipline and communication — not just equipment. The best MEWP in the world won't save you if your team skips the pre-brief.
Week 14 · Mar 2025

Reading OTDR traces: the 5 events you'll see on every long-haul run

PW
Piotr Wróbel
Fibre Technician, Warsaw

On our Warsaw backbone installation we ran 22km of single-mode OS2 fibre. OTDR testing every run. Here are the 5 trace events you will always encounter — and what they mean.

1
Launch event — Always appears at the start. Ignore it. It's the connector reflection from the OTDR port itself.
2
Connector loss events — Spikes on the trace. Should be <0.3dB. Above that: check the ferrule, clean the connector, re-test.
3
Fusion splice events — Tiny steps on the trace. Good splices are <0.02dB. We aim for 0.01dB or better on long-haul. Anything over 0.1dB gets re-spliced.
4
Fresnel reflections — Tall sharp spikes. Indicates an open end or air gap at a connector. Common at the far end of the cable. Expected — but watch for them mid-run (could be a break).
5
Noise floor — The flat line after the end event. If your signal drops into the noise floor before the end, your total loss is too high — check each event in order.
Key takeaway: Print every OTDR trace. File it with the as-built docs. When something fails in 3 years, that baseline trace is the first thing the client will ask for.
